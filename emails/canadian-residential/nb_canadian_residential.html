We're writing today to make you aware of a significant data exposure affecting Canadians. A threat actor has been distributing an alleged residential phone database containing approximately 10 million records belonging to Canadian residents.


If your personal information was detected as part of this breach, you will have already received a dark web monitoring alert from Securizone. This email is to provide you with additional information about the breach, how criminals may use this information to target Canadians impacted by this breach, and how your Securizone subscription can help you respond and stay vigilant.

Who is affected?

Approximately 10 million records belonging to Canadian residents are part of this exposed database. The leaked information may include full names, home addresses, postal codes, phone numbers, cities, and provinces. This type of consolidated residential data is particularly valuable to scammers because the combination of accurate personal details — your name, address, and phone number together — allows them to make fraudulent calls and messages seem credible and difficult to dismiss.

What should you do to protect yourself?

We recommend taking the following steps to help protect your information and guard against fraud:

  1. Be alert for phone-based scams: This type of data exposure is frequently used to fuel voice phishing attacks. Be cautious of unexpected calls claiming to be from the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA), Service Canada, your bank, or law enforcement — especially if the caller cites personal details like your home address or postal code to seem legitimate. No government agency will demand immediate payment by gift card, cryptocurrency, or wire transfer.

  2. Talk to seniors in your family: Residential phone data is commonly used to target older Canadians. The "Grandparent Scam" — where a caller claims a loved one is in trouble and needs bail money — becomes far more convincing when the caller already knows your name and address. Let the seniors in your life know what to look out for.

  3. When in doubt, hang up and call back: If you receive a suspicious call from someone claiming to represent a bank, government agency, or service provider, hang up. Look up the official number on their website or the back of your card, and call them directly to verify.

  4. Watch for phishing emails and texts: Criminals who have your phone number and address may also attempt to reach you by email or text. Be cautious of unexpected messages that reference your personal details, request information, or urge you to act quickly. If something feels off, don't click any links.

  5. Monitor your accounts and statements: Review your bank and credit card statements regularly for any activity you don't recognize, and check any alerts from your Securizone account.
